# Design Insights from the Literature Review

Research-informed ideas for how to build the Navigator Digitaler Wandel, drawn from the 21-search literature review (April 2026).

---

## The SELFIE Benchmark

SELFIE is the closest existing tool — psychometrically validated (Costa et al., 2021), used by 5.5M+ users in 80 countries. Its strength is multi-stakeholder collective reflection. Its limitation: it's fundamentally a Likert-scale survey that measures rather than provokes thinking. The Kompass framework's reflection questions are already more thought-provoking by design — the Navigator should lean hard into that differentiation.

---

## Three Core Design Ideas

### 1. Perspective-Switching as a Core Mechanic

Kampylis et al. (2023) showed that school leaders, teachers, and students perceive digital capacity very differently. Rauseo et al. (2022) found the same in Swiss vocational schools: management rated strategy higher, teachers rated culture higher. That gap between perspectives is where genuine insight happens.

**Navigator implementation ideas:**
- Let a teacher answer reflection questions, then show anonymized responses from school leaders at other schools on the same topic
- Prompt: "How do you think your school leader would answer this?" — cognitive dissonance drives self-awareness
- In team mode, surface divergence between group members' responses as the key output

### 2. The Barrier Lens

Gkrimpizi et al.'s taxonomy (2023, 149 citations) identifies barriers at three levels: teacher-level, school-level, and system-level. People already know transformation is important; what they lack is clarity about what specifically is blocking progress.

**Navigator implementation ideas:**
- Offer a mode where instead of walking through dimensions linearly, users explore "what's getting in the way?"
- More actionable than "where do you stand?" and aligns with fostering awareness rather than scoring
- Could map barriers to specific Kompass topics, creating targeted reflection paths

### 3. Connection-Based Navigation

Timotheou et al.'s review (2022, 544 citations) maps how infrastructure, policy, leadership, and teacher competence all interact. You can't meaningfully reflect on "Lernplattformen" without touching "Support" and "Anwendungskompetenzen."

**Navigator implementation ideas:**
- After reflecting on one topic, suggest the most relevant connected topic based on what the user said
- Use the cross-links in the Kompass framework as a navigation graph, not a linear sequence
- Let the conversation/reflection flow organically through the network rather than marching 1–35

## Additional Research-Informed Ideas

### Quick Pulse Mode

Cachia et al. (2023) showed you can capture meaningful signal with just 8 items instead of the full SELFIE instrument. The Navigator could offer a "quick pulse" mode (5–10 minutes, a few questions per dimension) alongside the deep dive — useful for team settings where you want participation without a 45-minute commitment.

### Self-Determination Theory for UX

Chiu et al. (2024) found that reflection tools work better when they support:
- **Autonomy** — choice in what to explore (let users pick their own path)
- **Competence** — feeling of learning something ("aha moment" insights)
- **Relatedness** — seeing how your situation connects to others (benchmarking, anonymized comparisons)

### AI as a Cross-Cutting Theme

Ng et al. (2023) and Tenberga et al. (2024) show AI competencies becoming a distinct category alongside digital competencies. The Kompass framework's 35 topics don't explicitly cover AI yet. Options:
- Add AI-specific reflection questions to existing topics (e.g., under "Mediendidaktik" or "Haltungen")
- Treat AI as a cross-cutting theme that surfaces across multiple dimensions
- This keeps the Navigator future-proof as AI integration accelerates

### Equity-Conscious Design

Castano-Munoz et al. (2022) found that policy-promoted SELFIE adoption disproportionately attracts already-advanced schools (Matthew effect). Design implications:
- Make the entry point low-friction and non-threatening
- Avoid language that implies schools "should" be at a certain level
- Consider how to reach schools that feel behind — they need the tool most but are least likely to seek it out
- The "not an assessment" framing is a key advantage here
